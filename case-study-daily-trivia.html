Case Study · Step · Retention

Turning onboarding dead time into a habit-forming experience

Teen users were stuck waiting for parent sponsorship with nothing to do in the app, leading to drop-off during a critical onboarding moment. I designed a lightweight, habit-forming trivia experience that kept users engaged throughout.

+19% Sponsorship conversion (7-day)
+23% Reduction in churn
2.5x App opens in first week
Role
Senior Product Designer (Acting Product Lead)
Scope
Product strategy, design, and execution end-to-end
Context

Most users entered the app with almost no functionality

At Step, users under 18 require a parent sponsor to unlock the app's core features. But the reality of that waiting period was far worse than expected.

11% Approved immediately
53% Waited more than 7 days
29% Never reopened after sending request
Problem

Waiting = churn

During the sponsorship waiting period, users had very limited functionality, no meaningful actions to take, and no reason to return. The data confirmed what the experience felt like.

  • Low re-engagement after sending the sponsor request
  • High drop-off during onboarding
  • A missed opportunity to convert highly motivated users

Why this mattered

We were losing a large portion of users at a critical moment. High intent users were arriving, hitting a wall, and churning before ever experiencing the product's value.

Insight

Users weren't just waiting — they were losing momentum

From user interviews, one theme stood out clearly: teens weren't just interested in using the product, they wanted to learn about money.

If we can't give users full functionality yet, we can still give them value.

Solution

Daily Trivia — a lightweight experience that keeps users coming back

A daily financial literacy trivia feature that gave users a meaningful reason to open the app every day during the waiting period, while reinforcing Step's educational mission.

How it works

The core experience was intentionally simple: one new question per day, multiple choice format, with immediate feedback and an explanation. Reinforcement came through streaks, points, and a weekly leaderboard with both global and friends rankings. A social layer invited users to compete with friends, naturally generating referrals.

Behaviour design

Built as a habit loop

This feature was intentionally designed around the four components of habit formation. Every element was chosen to reinforce a daily return.

Trigger
Daily question + reminders

A new question every day created a natural cue to open the app, supported by push notifications.

Action
Answer trivia

A single, frictionless interaction kept the barrier to engage extremely low.

Reward
Streaks, points, ranking

Immediate feedback, visible progress, and competitive standing gave users a reason to care.

Investment
Return daily to maintain progress

The more users engaged, the more they had to lose by stopping. Streaks created commitment.

Users reported
  • Being highly motivated to maintain their streaks
  • Remembering and sharing questions with friends
  • Returning daily out of habit, not obligation
Product thinking

Lightweight by design, strategic by intent

This wasn't just an engagement feature. It was a deliberate choice to solve the waiting period problem without over-engineering it.

Instead of
Sending more push reminders
Building a heavy education platform
We chose
A lightweight, iterative feature
Immediate value with low friction
Fast experimentation and learning

From nagging users to giving them a reason to return.

Design decisions

Principled tradeoffs

i

Build lightweight first

Prioritised speed and iteration over completeness. Started with a simple MVP — one question, one streak — then expanded progressively based on what was working.

ii

Balance fun and value

Made the experience feel game-like without losing the financial education purpose that made it meaningful to users and aligned with Step's mission.

iii

Progressive rollout

Shipped in four stages: MVP with question and streak, then global leaderboard, then rewards, then friends leaderboard. Each stage validated before the next was built.

Challenges

Overcoming skepticism

The biggest challenge wasn't design — it was getting the space to build it.

i

Stakeholder skepticism

There were real concerns about trivia being "non-essential" and pressure toward building a heavier, more formal education platform instead.

ii

Making the case

Positioned it as a low-cost experiment, framed as an engagement and growth lever, and partnered with marketing to support the broader strategy. The data made the argument for me.

Reflection

What I learned

01

Onboarding isn't just about guiding users — it's about sustaining motivation when progress is blocked. Value delivery can't wait for full functionality.

02

Lightweight experiences can outperform heavy solutions. A simple daily question drove more engagement than a full education platform would have.

03

Giving value beats asking for action. Engagement that serves the user first creates the conditions for conversion, not the other way around.
