Case Study · Step · Onboarding

Turning onboarding from a dead end into a guided system

Users were dropping into the app after signup with no clear direction, leading to confusion and churn. I designed a modular onboarding system that guided users through key steps based on their progress.

+31% KYC Completion (7-day)
+24% MTU Conversion (7-day)
Role
Senior Product Designer (Acting PM)
Scope
End-to-end ownership, systems design
Context

Users arrived in the app incomplete and unprepared

At Step, users could skip parts of the signup flow and land in the app without finishing critical setup steps. Even users who completed signup fully still entered the app with no clear next step, no understanding of how to unlock value, and no sense of progress.

Problem

Users were lost immediately after signup

Clickstream data told a clear story: users weren't refusing to engage, they just didn't know what to do.

  • Entering flows → backing out repeatedly
  • Navigating aimlessly across the app
  • Failing to complete critical steps like identity verification
"I didn't know what I was supposed to do."
"I haven't funded my account yet… I didn't know I had to."
"I thought that button was an ad."

The existing solution wasn't working

A legacy onboarding system was already in place, but it was effectively invisible.

  • Looked like an ad, consistently ignored
  • Showed only one task at a time
  • Forced a rigid, linear flow
  • Never explained why tasks mattered

Users had intent, but no direction.

Insight

Users weren't avoiding onboarding — they were searching for direction

They had goals and motivation, but didn't know how to get there. The problem wasn't friction; it was the absence of a clear path.

Users don't need fewer steps — they need a clearer path.

Solution

Account Progress: a system that guides users from signup to activation

A structured, visible progress system that appears immediately on the home screen and presents a clear list of tasks, grouped into In Progress and Up Next.

Smart progression

The system was designed around a key psychological insight: starting at 50% completion makes the goal feel achievable rather than daunting. The highest-friction step (identity verification) was placed at the end of the first task set, after trust had been established.

Systems thinking

Built to scale, not just ship

This wasn't designed as a one-off feature. The goal was a reusable system: modular task cards that any team could adopt to guide users through any critical flow.

Adoption
  • Adopted by the credit monitoring team
  • Will be adopted by our lending team, once the feature rolls out.
  • Established a company-wide scalable pattern
Design decisions

Principled tradeoffs

i

Prioritization

Focused only on high-impact steps. Every task in the system had to justify its presence by directly connecting to first transaction completion.

ii

Order strategy

Worked backward from the goal of first transaction. Tasks were sequenced to build trust before introducing friction.

iii

Reuse over specificity

Designed the system for reuse across teams, accepting some visual trade-offs in exchange for long-term scalability and consistency.

Challenges

Navigating resistance and competing priorities

Shipping a system, not just a screen, required stakeholder buy-in that wasn't always available.

i

Stakeholder resistance to scope

Designing for reuse meant pushing back on pressure to ship something faster and narrower. Making the case for the long-term investment required framing it in business terms.

ii

Balancing UX with business priorities

Some task ordering decisions were influenced by business goals rather than ideal user flow. The system had to accommodate both without undermining either.

Reflection

What I learned

01

Motivation is finite, so guide users while you have it. The window after signup is the highest-intent moment in the product.

02

Guidance increases trust and conversion. Users don't resist steps; they resist uncertainty. A clear path removes that friction.

03

Systems scale better than one-off features. Designing for reuse from the start created compounding value across multiple teams.
